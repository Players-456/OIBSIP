Biography

From Rameswaram
to the Stars

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam was born on 15 October 1931 in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, into a modest Tamil Muslim family. Despite humble beginnings, his insatiable curiosity and passion for learning set him apart from an early age.

He graduated in Physics from Saint Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli, and earned an aerospace engineering degree from the Madras Institute of Technology. What followed was a career that would reshape India's technological destiny.

As a scientist at DRDO and ISRO, Kalam played a pivotal role in India's civilian space programme and military missile development. He led projects such as SLV-III, Agni, Prithvi, and spearheaded Operation Shakti — India's 1998 nuclear tests — earning him the title "Missile Man of India."

Early Life

A Boy Who Sold Newspapers

Young Kalam helped his family financially by selling newspapers before school. His father, Jainulabdeen — a boat owner — instilled in him discipline and faith. His mother, Ashiamma, taught him generosity.


Inspired by his fifth-grade teacher Subramania Iyer, who showed him a bird in flight to explain aerodynamics, Kalam fell in love with science — a love that would carry him to the very edge of space.


From 2002 to 2007, as India's 11th President, he remained the "People's President" — visiting schools, inspiring students, and dreaming of a developed India by 2020. He passed away on 27 July 2015, doing what he loved most: addressing students at IIM Shillong.

Contributions

What He Gave to India

A lifetime of dedication across science, defence, space, and the human spirit.

🚀
Space Programme
Led India's first indigenous Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III), successfully placing the Rohini satellite in orbit in 1980 and establishing India as a major spacefaring nation.
⚔️
Missile Technology
Directed the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP), developing Agni, Prithvi, Akash, Trishul, and Nag missiles — giving India strategic self-reliance in defence.
⚛️
Nuclear Capability
Played a key role in Operation Shakti (Pokhran-II, 1998), India's successful nuclear tests that established India as a nuclear weapons state and a strategic power on the global stage.
🎓
Education & Youth
As President and after, Kalam relentlessly inspired millions of students. His Vision 2020 plan outlined pathways for India to become a developed nation through education, innovation, and technology.
📖
Literature & Thought
Authored transformative books including Wings of Fire, Ignited Minds, India 2020, and My Journey, reaching millions of readers and shaping a generation's ambition.
🏥
Healthcare Innovation
Collaborated with cardiologist Dr. Soma Raju to develop a low-cost coronary stent called the "Kalam-Raju Stent," making cardiac treatment more accessible to rural India.

Eternal Legacy

A Flame That
Never Dies

Dr. Kalam's life was a testament that where you come from does not define where you can go. His legacy burns across every laboratory, classroom, and launch pad in India.

The APJ Abdul Kalam Island (formerly Wheeler Island) — India's premier missile testing facility, renamed in his honour.

Over 30 universities, schools, and institutions across India bear his name — ensuring his spirit guides every student.

His books have sold millions of copies worldwide, translated into dozens of languages — igniting minds across continents.

29 July is observed as World Students' Day by the United Nations in honour of his birthday — 15 October 1931.

"The missile man who became a teacher" — his greatest pride was not rockets, but the young minds he inspired.
